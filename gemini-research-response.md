Architectural Strategies for Multi-Path UDP Bonding: Resolving Capacity Discovery and Scheduling Dependencies in Live Cellular Video Transport
Introduction to Heterogeneous Cellular Bonding and Transport Protocols
The transmission of real-time, high-definition video over mobile networks requires transport architectures capable of enduring extreme bandwidth volatility, unpredictable jitter, and frequent packet loss. In professional broadcasting contexts—such as the operational environments of mobile live-streaming backpacks like Teradek Bond or LiveU—a single cellular link is rarely sufficient to guarantee the stringent Quality of Experience (QoE) parameters demanded by live media. To resolve this, systems employ multi-path bonding, an architectural approach that aggregates multiple heterogeneous wireless links (spanning diverse carriers, 4G LTE, 5G mmWave, and Wi-Fi) into a single logical transport tunnel.
Historically, Multipath TCP (MPTCP) served as the foundational standard for multihomed devices seeking to aggregate bandwidth and increase resilience.1 MPTCP modifies the traditional TCP stack by establishing multiple subflows across available network interfaces, presenting a seamless, single-socket abstraction to the application layer. However, the rigidities of kernel-space implementations, coupled with TCP's strict in-order delivery requirements and head-of-line (HoL) blocking issues, have proven highly detrimental to latency-sensitive video streaming.3 Consequently, the industry is rapidly transitioning toward user-space, UDP-based multipath protocols, most notably Multipath QUIC (MPQUIC) and proprietary UDP bonding protocols tailored for media transport.5
Designing a proprietary multi-path UDP bonding system in Rust introduces profound algorithmic challenges, specifically regarding the interaction between congestion control and packet scheduling. A standard approach involves outfitting each physical link with a Bottleneck Bandwidth and Round-trip propagation time (BBR)-derived congestion controller (e.g., a "BiscayController") to estimate the available path capacity (btl_bw), followed by a Deficit Weighted Round Robin (DWRR) scheduler that distributes video packets across the links proportionally to these capacity estimates. While logically sound, this architecture inherently creates a systemic feedback loop—a "chicken-and-egg" capacity discovery problem.
This comprehensive research report dissects this paradox, evaluates the fundamental trade-offs between rate-based (DWRR) and congestion window (cwnd)-based scheduling paradigms, and analyzes how advanced MPTCP schedulers (minRTT, BLEST, ECF) address path heterogeneity. Furthermore, it investigates applied methodologies from industry leaders—such as Apple's MPQUIC implementations and Alibaba's XLINK—and synthesizes state-of-the-art research on active capacity probing (including Dejavu, MultSched, and packet-pair techniques) to provide a definitive architectural blueprint for low-latency, multi-path video transport.
The BBR Capacity Discovery Paradox: The "Chicken-and-Egg" Problem
The Mechanics of BBR and btl_bw Estimation
To fully understand the capacity discovery problem in a proportionally scheduled multipath system, it is necessary to deconstruct the operational mechanics of the BBR congestion control algorithm. Unlike traditional loss-based algorithms (such as NewReno or CUBIC) that infer congestion from dropped packets, BBR models the network path by continuously estimating two primary parameters: the bottleneck bandwidth (btl_bw) and the two-way round-trip propagation delay (RTprop).7
BBR calculates the btl_bw by measuring the actual delivery rate of data over a moving window of recent round trips.8 The delivery rate is computed simply as the amount of data acknowledged by the receiver divided by the time elapsed between the transmission of that data and the receipt of the acknowledgment. Therefore, btl_bw is not an abstract measurement of the physical medium's theoretical maximum; it is a direct measurement of the rate at which the network successfully delivered the data it was provided.
The Scheduler-Controller Feedback Loop
The paradox manifests when the BBR congestion controller is tightly coupled with a DWRR scheduler that relies on btl_bw to determine traffic distribution weights. Because BBR can only measure the delivery rate of the traffic it is actually given to transmit, its capacity estimate is strictly bounded by the input rate dictated by the scheduler.9
Consider a scenario where a bonded system aggregates two cellular modems: Link A, which possesses a true physical capacity of 20 Mbps, and Link B, which possesses a true physical capacity of 5 Mbps. The live video encoder is producing a steady stream of 8 Mbps.
Initial State: Operating under an initial state of uncertainty, the DWRR scheduler assigns equal weights to both links, splitting the 8 Mbps video stream perfectly in half. Link A receives 4 Mbps of traffic, and Link B receives 4 Mbps.
Estimation Phase: The BBR controllers on both links transmit their assigned 4 Mbps. Because 4 Mbps is well within the physical limits of both the 20 Mbps Link A and the 5 Mbps Link B, all packets are successfully delivered. The BBR controllers calculate their delivery rates and report btl_bw estimates of approximately 4 Mbps for both links.
The Trap: The DWRR scheduler queries the controllers, observes that both Link A and Link B report a capacity of 4 Mbps, and concludes that the links are entirely equal. It therefore maintains the 50/50 traffic split.
In this scenario, Link A's true capacity of 20 Mbps remains entirely undiscovered, while Link B operates perilously close to its maximum limit, risking congestion, queuing delay, and jitter.9 The system is trapped in a stable but sub-optimal equilibrium. The scheduler relies on the controller to reveal capacity, but the controller relies on the scheduler to provide enough data to probe that capacity.
The Failure of PROBE_BW in Multipath Contexts
BBR is designed to probe for additional bandwidth during its PROBE_BW state cycle. During this phase, BBR applies a pacing gain (typically 1.25) to its sending rate, momentarily transmitting faster than its current btl_bw estimate to observe if the delivery rate increases commensurately. If the network successfully absorbs the higher rate, BBR updates its btl_bw upward.
However, in a multi-path context governed by a rate-limited video encoder and a DWRR scheduler, this pacing gain mechanism is rendered ineffective. The 1.25 pacing gain only pushes against the localized token bucket or pacing queue of that specific subflow; it does not instruct the overarching DWRR scheduler to allocate more of the aggregate application traffic to that link. Because the aggregate traffic is finite, the localized BBR instance starves for data during the PROBE_BW phase. It attempts to pace faster, but there are no packets in its local queue to send. Consequently, the delivery rate does not increase, the btl_bw estimate stagnates, and the true physical capacity remains hidden.
Trade-offs: Congestion Window (cwnd)-Based vs. Rate-Based Scheduling
The capacity discovery problem inherent in rate-based systems is organically solved by traditional Congestion Window (cwnd)-based multipath protocols. MPTCP implementations leverage specialized congestion control algorithms such as the Linked Increase Algorithm (LIA), Opportunistic LIA (OLIA), and Balanced Linked Adaptation (BALIA) to manage multiple cwnds simultaneously.11 However, evaluating whether to adopt a cwnd-based or rate-based approach requires a rigorous analysis of their impacts on latency-sensitive real-time video.
Mechanics and Strengths of cwnd-Based Scheduling
In a cwnd-based multipath system, each subflow maintains a distinct congestion window that strictly governs the maximum number of unacknowledged bytes allowed in flight across that specific link. The multipath scheduling logic is elegantly simple: the scheduler iterates through available subflows and pushes packets onto any subflow that currently possesses available space in its cwnd.3
This architecture inherently solves the capacity discovery problem. If a subflow represents a high-capacity, low-latency path, its packets will be delivered and acknowledged rapidly. These rapid acknowledgments continuously slide the window forward, freeing up cwnd space.3 The scheduler naturally routes more packets to this fast link simply because it consistently presents available window space. When the network is uncongested, the TCP/MPTCP congestion avoidance mechanisms (e.g., LIA or OLIA) additively increase the cwnd, continuously pushing more data into the network to discover the upper bounds of the path's capacity.11
Furthermore, coupled congestion control algorithms like OLIA and BALIA ensure that the total bandwidth consumed by the MPTCP connection is fair to competing single-path TCP flows at bottleneck links, dynamically shifting traffic away from congested paths and toward uncongested ones based purely on cwnd dynamics and packet loss signals.11
The Bufferbloat Penalty for Live Media
Despite solving the capacity discovery dilemma, the fatal flaw of cwnd-based scheduling for live, latency-sensitive video over cellular networks is its inherent reliance on queue accumulation. Loss-based cwnd controllers discover link capacity by continuously expanding the congestion window until the network bottleneck buffer overflows, resulting in a dropped packet.10
In modern LTE and 5G cellular networks, base stations and core network gateways are configured with excessively deep buffers designed to prevent packet loss during inevitable radio link fluctuations and handovers. When a cwnd-based controller relentlessly fills these deep buffers to probe capacity, it induces a phenomenon known as bufferbloat.14
For real-time video, end-to-end latency must remain strictly bounded. For highly interactive applications, such as remote drone piloting, live broadcasting, or virtual reality (VR), latencies must often remain below 130 milliseconds to prevent noticeable lag or motion sickness.13 As the cwnd grows to probe capacity, the deep cellular buffers fill, and queuing delay skyrockets. Video frames become trapped in base station queues, arriving at the receiver too late for the decoder's playback deadline. This results in frame drops, severe jitter, video freezing, and a catastrophic degradation of the QoE.10
The Pacing Advantage of Rate-Based Scheduling
Rate-based scheduling, utilizing algorithms like BBR and DWRR, deliberately decouples the packet sending rate from the accumulation of in-flight data. By strictly pacing the transmission of packets based on the estimated btl_bw and utilizing the calculated RTprop, BBR ensures that the bottleneck queue remains nearly empty, driving queuing delay toward the theoretical minimum.19
This pacing is profoundly advantageous for bursty video traffic. Video encoders output data in discrete, highly variable-sized frames (I, P, and B frames). An I-frame (intra-coded picture) contains full image data and is vastly larger than subsequent predictive frames. If a massive I-frame is instantly dumped into a cwnd-based socket with an open window, it creates a sudden micro-burst that fills the network buffer and spikes instantaneous latency. A rate-based system smooths this burst over the inter-frame interval through strict microsecond-level pacing, preserving stringent latency bounds.10

Capacity Discovery and Path Selection in Advanced MPTCP Schedulers
To optimize multipath transport for latency-sensitive applications and mitigate the inefficiencies of path heterogeneity, researchers have developed specialized schedulers that move far beyond simple round-robin or default window-filling strategies. Analyzing how these MPTCP schedulers handle capacity and timing provides critical algorithmic logic that can be adapted into a Rust-based bonded UDP system.
The Default Baseline: minRTT and Head-of-Line Blocking
The default packet scheduler in standard Linux MPTCP implementations is minRTT (Shortest RTT First). Given multiple subflows that have available cwnd space, minRTT strictly prioritizes the subflow with the lowest measured Round Trip Time.3 While intuitively this minimizes the propagation latency of individual packets, it triggers severe performance degradation in highly asymmetric networks.
Consider a mobile device bonded across a high-speed, low-latency 5G link (Path A) and a slower, high-latency 4G LTE link (Path B). The minRTT scheduler will route all video packets over Path A until Path A's congestion window is entirely exhausted. At that exact moment, the scheduler immediately begins pushing packets onto the slower Path B.4 Because Path B has a significantly higher propagation delay, the packets sent on Path B will arrive long after subsequent packets that are eventually sent on Path A (once Path A's cwnd opens up again after receiving acknowledgments).
At the receiving end, the transport layer must reorder these packets into their correct sequential order before delivering them to the video application. The delayed packets from Path B create a blockade in the receiver's reassembly buffer. This out-of-order arrival phenomenon creates Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking, stalling the video decoder, inflating application-level latency, and potentially causing the receiver buffer to overflow.9
The Redundant Scheduler
For environments demanding absolute minimum latency and maximum reliability, multipath protocols can employ Redundant scheduling (often referred to as ReMP or concurrent duplicate transmission). Redundant schedulers completely ignore capacity distribution and instead transmit identical, duplicate copies of every video packet across all available cellular links simultaneously.4
The receiver processes whichever packet arrives first and discards the duplicate. Redundant scheduling achieves the theoretical minimum latency possible across a bonded array and inherently bypasses both the capacity discovery problem and the HoL blocking problem, as the fast path will always deliver the data first. However, this approach halves the spectral efficiency of the system and significantly increases cellular data costs. While viable for low-bitrate critical telemetry or VoIP, duplicating a 10 Mbps live 1080p video stream is financially and computationally prohibitive for most broadcasting applications.22
BLEST (Blocking Estimation) Scheduler
The BLock ESTimation (BLEST) scheduler proactively addresses the HoL blocking problem inherent in the minRTT approach by estimating whether the utilization of a slow subflow will ultimately harm the application's overall delivery latency.4
BLEST introduces a predictive decision window based on cwnd drain rates. When the primary, low-latency path reaches its cwnd limit, BLEST does not immediately shift traffic to the secondary, high-latency path. Instead, it evaluates the state of the fast path's in-flight packets. BLEST calculates an estimate of the time it will take for the fast path's acknowledgments to arrive, which would reopen the cwnd.4
If the estimated time to simply wait for the fast path to become available again, plus the transmission RTT of the fast path, is less than the time it would take to immediately transmit the packet over the slow path, BLEST intentionally pauses transmission. It willingly underutilizes the slower path to wait for the optimal path.4 By doing so, BLEST drastically reduces receiver buffer starvation and HoL blocking, making it highly effective for interactive, latency-sensitive applications operating over asymmetric cellular links.9

Machine Learning, MultSched, and ES-MPTCP
Emerging research into multipath scheduling has begun integrating deep reinforcement learning (DRL) and multi-objective optimization to dynamically adapt scheduling weights in highly volatile environments. Studies investigating frameworks like "MultSched" and Deep Q-Learning (DQL) based MPTCP demonstrate that reinforcement learning agents can autonomously learn the optimal balance between throughput and latency by observing the continuous state space of RTT, loss rates, and buffer occupancies.11
Furthermore, algorithms like ES-MPTCP (Energy-Saving MPTCP) highlight the necessity of considering physical hardware constraints in multi-cellular environments.30 Activating multiple LTE/5G modems simultaneously consumes massive amounts of battery power. ES-MPTCP designs a target optimization function that shifts traffic away from energy-intensive paths during periods of adequate bandwidth, only re-engaging them when the primary path's performance degrades.30 For portable video broadcasting backpacks, integrating power-awareness into the DWRR scheduling logic ensures operational longevity without sacrificing video stability.
Alternative Capacity Estimation Approaches for Real-Time Media
Because the BBR btl_bw parameter is inextricably linked to the DWRR scheduler's input, a multi-path UDP system must implement out-of-band methods to discover true link capacity. Active capacity probing decouples bandwidth estimation from the application's video data scheduling, intentionally injecting measurement traffic to reveal the physical limits of the cellular links.
Packet-Pair and Packet-Train Dispersion Techniques
One of the most theoretically robust and well-documented methods for active capacity discovery independent of cwnd or application rate limits is the packet-pair dispersion technique.23 This methodology involves the sender intentionally injecting two dedicated probe packets—typically sized to match the path's Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU)—back-to-back into the network link.
Because the packets are injected sequentially with effectively zero temporal interval at the sender, they travel together until they encounter the network's bottleneck link.23 At the bottleneck (e.g., the cellular base station's radio resource scheduler or the local device's modem queue), the first packet must be fully transmitted over the physical medium before the second packet can begin its transmission. The primary mathematical challenge of packet-pair probing is cross-traffic interference; external traffic from other cellular users can interleave between the probe packets, causing temporal compression or expansion of the pair.31 Therefore, the Rust system must collect multiple dispersion samples over time and apply robust statistical filtering (such as a moving median or kernel density estimation) to derive a stable capacity metric.
Industry Implementations for Latency-Sensitive Traffic
Analyzing how major technology corporations handle multipath architectures provides highly pragmatic, battle-tested methodologies that inform custom system design.
Apple's MPQUIC Interactive Mode
Apple's implementation of Multipath TCP and Multipath QUIC (MPQUIC) within the iOS Darwin networking stack provides a highly relevant case study for managing latency-sensitive traffic on multihomed mobile devices.33 Through extensive telemetry, Apple identified that naive bandwidth aggregation across heterogeneous links frequently degraded performance rather than improving it. Consequently, Apple explicitly categorizes multipath behavior into three operational modes: Handover, Aggregate, and Interactive.33
For latency-critical applications, such as the Siri voice assistant, Apple mandates the Interactive Mode. Crucially, the Interactive Mode does not attempt to aggregate bandwidth across Wi-Fi and Cellular links to maximize throughput.33 It operates on a strict primary/backup scheduling paradigm. The packet scheduler designates the lowest-latency path (typically Wi-Fi) as the primary subflow and the cellular path exclusively as a standby backup.33
The scheduler sends data exclusively on the primary path. The secondary path is only activated if a specific, critical degradation condition is met on the primary link, such as a Retransmission Timeout (RTO) or severe RTT inflation.34 When utilizing MPQUIC, this strategy is vastly superior to MPTCP. Because QUIC manages data in independent streams with isolated packet number spaces, the protocol can instantly re-inject lost data on the backup cellular path without stalling the entire connection byte-stream due to HoL blocking.33
For a Rust-based live video bonding system, Apple's architectural decision highlights a vital principle: for strictly latency-critical data (such as video I-frames or audio synchronizations), strict path prioritization or immediate re-injection over a secondary link is often more effective than attempting complex proportional DWRR aggregation across highly asymmetric cellular links.
Alibaba's XLINK: QoE-Driven MPQUIC
Alibaba's XLINK represents a state-of-the-art deployment of MPQUIC tailored specifically for large-scale, short-video e-commerce applications (Taobao).5 Serving millions of users, XLINK directly addresses the limitations of kernel-space multipath implementations by integrating the transport protocol directly with the application's video player in user space.5
XLINK overcomes the capacity discovery and latency dilemma through several innovative mechanisms that bypass the standard minRTT or cwnd limitations:
Active Probing on Idle Paths: To maintain an accurate estimate of path latency and capacity without wasting expensive cellular data or generating massive packet-train overhead, the XLINK state manager actively probes backup paths by periodically sending minuscule 8-byte PING frames at 50ms intervals.37 This allows the scheduler to maintain continuous awareness of the cellular path's RTT condition without impacting the DWRR weights of the main video stream.
Packet Re-injection Mode: Rather than relying purely on predictive scheduling (like ECF) or massive buffer accumulation, XLINK embraces a proactive re-injection strategy to guarantee latency bounds.6 If a packet sent on a primary path is deemed delayed based on real-time RTT telemetry, XLINK immediately re-injects a duplicate of that packet on a secondary path.36 Because QUIC handles data multiplexing natively, the receiver accepts whichever packet arrives first and gracefully discards the duplicate. This effectively places an absolute upper bound on tail latency at the expense of a tightly controlled redundant traffic overhead (measured at roughly 2.1% in Alibaba's production environment).6
Application-Aware Priority: Because XLINK operates in user space, it is fully decoupled from opaque kernel buffers. It reads Quality of Experience (QoE) telemetry signals—such as the video player's buffer level and decoding frame rate—directly from the application.6 It adjusts multipath scheduling dynamically based on whether the video currently requires high throughput (to fill a depleting buffer) or low latency (to prevent an imminent stall).
Emerging Research in Bonded Cellular Transport: Dejavu
Furthering the optimization of real-time video, the Dejavu architecture (proposed by Pan Hu et al.) explores how prior knowledge of network states can enhance videoconferencing and streaming over cellular networks.17
Cellular networks, particularly dense 5G mmWave and congested Urban LTE environments, exhibit highly volatile physical layer parameters. Handover events, physical obstructions, and base station load balancing manifest as severe, unpredictable jitter at the transport layer.17 Traditional transport protocols react to these fluctuations after they occur. Dejavu models these network fluctuations geographically and temporally, utilizing machine learning techniques to "mask" the jitter by predicting bandwidth variations before they cause application-level stalls.17
While Dejavu focuses heavily on the interaction between the video codec and the receiver's jitter buffer, it underscores the necessity of predictive telemetry for capacity discovery. For a Rust-based bonded system, integrating predictive models (such as Long Short-Term Memory networks or simple historical heuristics) into the multipath scheduler allows the system to adjust DWRR weights based on predicted future capacity during handover events, rather than strictly relying on historical, lagging btl_bw metrics.17
Architectural Blueprint for a Rust-Based Multi-Path UDP System
Synthesizing the theoretical mechanisms of cwnd vs. rate-based protocols, the mathematical logic of the ECF and BLEST schedulers, and the practical, at-scale innovations of XLINK and Apple MPQUIC, the following architectural blueprint outlines the construction of a Rust-based multi-path UDP bonding system designed specifically to resolve the BBR capacity discovery paradox for live video.
1. Decoupling Capacity Discovery via the Active Prober Module
To solve the "chicken-and-egg" problem, the architecture must fundamentally sever the dependency between the DWRR scheduler's traffic allocation and the capacity estimator.
Implementation: The Rust system must leverage asynchronous runtimes (e.g., tokio) to implement an independent ActiveProber module that operates concurrently with the BiscayController (the BBR implementation) on each cellular link.
Mechanism: Instead of relying solely on btl_bw derived from the application's video data, the ActiveProber periodically injects high-priority packet trains (e.g., sequences of 4 back-to-back MTU-sized UDP datagrams) across all bonded cellular modems.23
Integration: The receiver acknowledges these probes with high-resolution microsecond timestamps. The sender calculates the dispersion and derives an absolute upper-bound capacity limit. The DWRR scheduler then uses this out-of-band active estimate—rather than BBR's constrained delivery rate—to set its proportional distribution weights. This allows the scheduler to accurately balance traffic and scale up underutilized links, breaking the false equilibrium.
2. Predictive DWRR with ECF and BLEST Logic
A naive proportional DWRR scheduler is insufficient for live video because it ignores path RTT, inevitably leading to Head-of-Line blocking over heterogeneous cellular links. The Rust DWRR scheduler must be enhanced with predictive mathematical logic derived from the Earliest Completion First (ECF) and BLEST algorithms.3
Implementation: Before the central scheduler assigns a video frame to a specific link's queue, it evaluates the available capacity and RTT of all links.
Adaptation: Using the adapted ECF formula , the scheduler assesses whether routing a packet to a slower, high-latency cellular link will delay the overall frame assembly compared to queueing it for the faster link.3 If the math indicates the slow link inflates the completion time, the scheduler dynamically overrides its DWRR weights to bypass the slow link, mimicking BLEST's proactive HoL blocking avoidance.9
3. QoE-Aware Asymmetric Routing and Re-injection
Drawing directly from Alibaba's XLINK and Apple's MPQUIC Interactive mode, the Rust system should abandon strict proportional load balancing for structurally critical video data.6
Implementation: In modern live video codecs (H.264/HEVC), I-frames (keyframes) and audio packets are paramount; their loss or delay stalls the entire decoding pipeline. The Rust system must implement a QoE-aware tagging system that parses the video stream prior to scheduling.36
Mechanism: For standard P and B predictive frames, the predictive DWRR scheduler distributes the load to maximize aggregate bandwidth.22 For critical I-frames and audio, the system utilizes a targeted re-injection strategy.24 If an I-frame packet sent on the primary link is not acknowledged within a tight threshold (e.g., ), the sender instantly re-injects a duplicate UDP datagram over the secondary cellular link. This prevents catastrophic video stalls without inducing the massive spectral overhead of full 1:1 redundancy.6
4. Asynchronous BBR Pacing Enforcement
Because the system is transporting live video, the latency benefits of rate-based pacing must be rigorously preserved to prevent bufferbloat in the 4G/5G base stations.
Implementation: The BiscayController must strictly pace packets into the kernel UDP sockets. Rather than relying on kernel-level tc-fq (Traffic Control Fair Queueing) which may be inaccessible in certain mobile OS environments, the Rust application should implement custom token-bucket timers using asynchronous primitives (e.g., tokio::time::sleep or high-precision wheel timers) to space packets accurately at the microsecond level.7
Conclusion
The pursuit of robust, low-latency live video transmission over bonded cellular networks requires navigating the highly complex interplay between transport layer capacity estimation and packet scheduling. Implementing a BBR-derived controller with a proportional DWRR scheduler inherently traps the system in a mathematical feedback loop, where capacity estimates merely reflect scheduled allocations rather than physical link realities. While transitioning to a congestion window (cwnd)-based scheduling model—as seen in traditional MPTCP implementations—resolves this capacity discovery paradox organically, it introduces unacceptable bufferbloat and latency spikes that cripple real-time video performance.
To break this deadlock, a modern user-space multi-path UDP system must hybridize the pacing benefits of rate-based congestion control with the predictive intelligence of advanced MPTCP schedulers. By implementing out-of-band active probing using packet-pair dispersion to discover true physical capacity, the system frees the DWRR scheduler from its localized feedback loop. Furthermore, adapting the mathematical logic of the Earliest Completion First (ECF) and BLEST schedulers allows the system to proactively avoid Head-of-Line blocking over asymmetric 4G/5G links. Ultimately, incorporating application-aware packet re-injection—mirroring the architectural successes of Apple's MPQUIC Interactive mode and Alibaba's XLINK—ensures that critical video frames survive network volatility, delivering a seamless, high-throughput, and rigorously latency-bounded Quality of Experience.
